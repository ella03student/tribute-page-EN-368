Audre Lorde

“BLACK, LESBIAN, MOTHER, WARRIOR, POET”

1934 - Audre Lorde was born on February 18 to Caribbean immigrants in New York City.

1930s - She had a strained relationship with her parents and turned to poetry as an emotional outlet. She also changed her name from “Audrey” to “Audre” for the sake of artistic symmetry with her last name.

1940s - Audre attended Hunter College High School (a high school for intellectually gifted students) but felt like an outcast due to her being "both crazy and queer.”

1954 - Lorde spent a year the National Autonomous University of Mexico, and she described it as a time of great personal growth and realization.

1961 - She got her master’s in library science from Columbia University, and worked as a librarian in Mount Vernon, New York.

1962 - Audre married attorney Edwin Rollins (a white, gay man) and had two children.

1968 - Lorde served as writer-in-residence at Tougaloo College in Mississippi and led workshops with her black undergraduate students. She also reaffirmed her identity as "crazy and queer" and vowed to live according to that.

1970 - She and Edwin Rollins divorced.

1970 to 1981 - Audre taught English at the John Jay College of Criminal Justice and fought for the creation of a black studies department there. She also helped found the Women's Coalition of St. Croix (an organization that supports survivors of sexual abuse).

1977 - She became an associate of the Women's Institute for Freedom of the Press, a non-profit aiming to increase communication between women and connect the public with women-based media.

1978 - Lorde was diagnosed with breast cancer and underwent a mastectomy.

1980 - She co-founded Kitchen Table: Women of Color Press (the first U.S. publisher for women of color) with Barbara Smith and Cherríe Moraga.

1981 - She met black feminist Dr. Gloria Joseph, who became her romantic life partner.

1984 - Audre started a visiting professorship in West Berlin at the Free University of Berlin and became a strong influence in the developing Afro-German movement there, even coining the term “Afro-German.” Her work inspired Dagmar Schultz’s film Audre Lorde: The Berlin Years 1984–1992. Lorde also found out her cancer had metastasized in her liver.

1989 - Just a month after the fall of the Berlin Wall, she wrote her poem "East Berlin 1989," stating her concern about the violent racism against Afro-Germans and black populations in Berlin.

1991 - Lorde was titled State Poet of New York.

1992 - Audre received the Bill Whitehead Award for Lifetime Achievement from Publishing Triangle. She also took the name Gamba Adisa during an African naming ceremony (means "Warrior: She Who Makes Her Meaning Known"). She passed away on November 17 from her breast cancer.

"What I leave behind has a life of its own. I've said this about poetry; I've said it about children. Well, in a sense I'm saying it about the very artifact of who I have been."

- Audre Lorde
